# Renal and Thyroid Panel Interpretation

## 1. Related Tests

* Creatinine
* TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)

---

## 2. What High Values May Suggest

### High Creatinine

* Reduced kidney function
* Acute kidney injury
* Chronic kidney disease
* Dehydration
* Urinary tract obstruction
* Increased muscle breakdown or high muscle mass
* Medication-related elevation

### High TSH

* Primary hypothyroidism
* Inadequate thyroid hormone replacement in treated patients
* Autoimmune thyroid disease (such as Hashimoto thyroiditis)

---

## 3. What Low Values May Suggest

### Low Creatinine

* Reduced muscle mass
* Malnutrition
* Advanced liver disease
* Pregnancy (physiologic decrease)

### Low TSH

* Hyperthyroidism
* Excess thyroid hormone replacement
* Secondary (central) hypothyroidism
* Suppression during severe non-thyroidal illness

---

## 4. Common Supporting Symptoms

### Kidney-Related

* Fatigue
* Weakness
* Swelling of the legs or face
* Reduced urine output
* Nausea
* Loss of appetite
* High blood pressure

### Thyroid-Related

* Fatigue
* Weight change
* Heat or cold intolerance
* Palpitations
* Tremor
* Constipation
* Dry skin
* Hair thinning
* Slow heart rate
* Anxiety or depression

---

## 5. Missing Evidence

* Additional laboratory tests to confirm kidney function and thyroid function
* Urinalysis when kidney disease is suspected
* Clinical history and medication review
* Physical examination
* Previous laboratory results to identify trends
* Relevant imaging studies when clinically indicated

---

## 6. Suggested Follow-up

* Repeat abnormal results if clinically appropriate
* Review symptoms and medical history
* Review current medications and supplements
* Assess hydration status
* Perform a focused physical examination
* Order additional confirmatory laboratory testing if indicated
* Consider referral to a nephrologist or endocrinologist for persistent or significant abnormalities

---

## 7. Safety Note

This document is intended for clinical decision support only. Laboratory results must be interpreted together with the patient's history, physical examination, and additional diagnostic findings. It does not provide a final diagnosis, does not replace physician judgment, and should not be used as the sole basis for treatment decisions. Critical or rapidly changing abnormalities require prompt medical evaluation.
