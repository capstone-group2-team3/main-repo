# CBC Interpretation

## 1. Related Tests

- Creatinine
- TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)

---

## 2. What High Values May Suggest

### High Creatinine
- Reduced kidney function
- Acute kidney injury
- Chronic kidney disease
- Dehydration
- Urinary tract obstruction
- Increased muscle breakdown or high muscle mass
- Medication-related elevation

### High TSH
- Primary hypothyroidism
- Inadequate thyroid hormone replacement
- Autoimmune thyroid disease (e.g., Hashimoto thyroiditis)

---

## 3. What Low Values May Suggest

### Low Creatinine
- Reduced muscle mass
- Malnutrition
- Advanced liver disease
- Pregnancy (physiologic decrease)

### Low TSH
- Hyperthyroidism
- Excess thyroid hormone replacement
- Secondary (central) hypothyroidism
- Suppression during severe non-thyroidal illness

---

## 4. Common Supporting Symptoms

### Kidney-Related
- Fatigue
- Weakness
- Swelling of the legs or face
- Reduced urine output
- Nausea
- Loss of appetite
- High blood pressure

### Thyroid-Related
- Fatigue
- Weight change
- Heat or cold intolerance
- Palpitations
- Tremor
- Constipation
- Dry skin
- Hair thinning
- Slow heart rate
- Anxiety or depression

---

## 5. Missing Evidence

- Additional laboratory tests to confirm kidney function and thyroid function
- Clinical history and medication review
- Physical examination
- Previous laboratory results to assess trends
- Relevant imaging studies when clinically indicated

---

## 6. Suggested Follow-up

- Repeat abnormal results if clinically appropriate
- Review symptoms and medical history
- Review medications and supplements
- Assess hydration status
- Perform a focused physical examination
- Order confirmatory testing if clinically indicated
- Consider referral to a nephrologist or endocrinologist for persistent abnormalities

---

## 7. Safety Note

This document is intended for clinical decision support only. Laboratory findings must be interpreted together with the patient's medical history, physical examination, and additional diagnostic information. It does not provide a final diagnosis, does not replace physician judgment, and should not be used as the sole basis for treatment decisions.
